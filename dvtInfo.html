Deep Vein Thrombosis Information

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) is usually the result of narrow blood veins, blood-clotting abnormalities, injuries or prolonged periods of inactivity such as a long airplane ride or lengthy bed rest. Symptoms of Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) include pain, swelling and discoloration around the area of the clot.

There are two types of DVT: Acute and chronic. The physicians at Utah Valley Interventional Associates (UVIA) are experts when it comes to treating both acute and chronic DVT. They can eliminate current clots and help avoid future one from forming.

DVT Treatment

Patients diagnosed with DVT are initially given anticoagulants. White anticoagulants stop more clots from forming they generally do little to dissolve the existing clot. Instead the physicians at UVIA use appropriate minimally invasive, interventions to eliminate clots.

One intervention is called Pharmacomechanical Thrombolysis. Under local anesthesia, a catheter is inserted into the vein. Using ultrasound guided imagery, the catheter is guided through the veins until it reaches the clot. Then medication is injected directly into the clot to dissolve it. This procedure generally takes about an hour to perform.

A second procedure breaks the clot into tiny pieces them removes them. Under local anesthesia, a flexible wire is guided to the clot ultrasound guided imagery. A catheter is then run along the wire. Small balloons are inflated in the vein on either side of the clot to keep the medication localized. Clot busting drugs are then applied to the clot to soften it – breaking it into tiny pieces. The balloons are deflated and the catheter’s vacuum then suctions the tiny pieces of blood clot out of the body. A temporary filter is installed preventing any bits from escaping into the blood stream.
